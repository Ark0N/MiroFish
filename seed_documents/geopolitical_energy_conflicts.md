# Geopolitical Conflicts Affecting Global Energy Supply — March 2026

## Executive Summary

As of March 22, 2026, the global energy system faces its most severe disruption since the 1973 Arab oil embargo. The convergence of the US-Israel war on Iran (launched February 28, 2026), the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the ongoing Russia-Ukraine war (now in its fifth year), and regional instability across Africa has removed approximately 10 million barrels per day (mb/d) of oil from global markets. Brent crude surged past $126/bbl at its peak, and the IEA has authorized the largest emergency reserves release in history — 400 million barrels. Qatar's LNG exports (20% of global supply) are partially offline, European TTF gas prices have spiked 93% from pre-crisis levels to €61.90/MWh, and Iraq has declared force majeure on all foreign-operated oilfields. The crisis is reshaping global energy trade flows, with Saudi Arabia rerouting up to 4.2 mb/d through Red Sea pipelines and India/China scrambling to secure Russian crude.

## 1. Active Conflicts Affecting Energy

### 1.1 The 2026 Iran War and Strait of Hormuz Crisis

#### Timeline and Background

- **Pre-war escalation**: Failed nuclear negotiations in Geneva and a prior 12-day air conflict in 2025 escalated tensions between Iran, the US, and Israel.
- **February 28, 2026**: The United States and Israel launched coordinated air strikes on Iran, assassinating Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. US forces have struck more than 7,000 targets in Iran since that date.
- **March 1, 2026**: First oil tanker (Skylight) attacked in the Strait of Hormuz off Oman's coast. Two Indian crew members killed, three injured.
- **March 2, 2026**: A senior IRGC official confirmed the Strait of Hormuz was closed and threatened any ship attempting passage. Iran launched retaliatory strikes on Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura refinery, Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex, and UAE's Mussafah fuel terminal.
- **March 2, 2026**: The 2026 Lebanon war began between Israel and Hezbollah. Over 1,000 killed in Lebanon, nearly 1 million displaced (19% of population). IDF launched "targeted ground operation" in southern Lebanon.
- **March 6, 2026**: A tugboat dispatched to assist the damaged vessel Safeen Prestige was struck by two missiles and sank; at least three crew missing.
- **March 8, 2026**: Brent crude surpassed $100/bbl for the first time in four years.
- **March 9, 2026**: Iran reportedly laid fewer than 10 naval mines in the Strait. Brent crude hit nearly $120/bbl.
- **March 11, 2026**: IEA member countries agreed to release 400 million barrels of emergency reserves — the largest such action in history.
- **March 12, 2026**: Iran made 21 confirmed attacks on merchant ships. Two tankers (Marshall Islands-flagged Safesea Vishnu and Malta-flagged Zefyros) attacked by Iranian drone boats off Port of Basra, Iraq. Iraq suspended all oil terminal operations. Six vessels attacked total with reports of Iranian drone boats and sea mines.
- **March 13, 2026**: US rolled back Russia oil sanctions with a 30-day waiver on ~155 million barrels in floating storage/transit.
- **March 16, 2026**: Iran struck the UAE's Shah gas field (drone attack set it ablaze) and Fujairah Oil Industrial Zone. The Shah field supplies ~20% of UAE gas supply and 5% of global granulated sulphur.
- **March 18, 2026**: Israel unilaterally struck Iran's South Pars gas field and Asaluyeh oil refinery. South Pars provides ~70% of Iran's gas production; initial reports indicated ~12% of Iran's total gas production was damaged. A projectile hit a structure 350 meters from the Bushehr nuclear reactor. Iran retaliated by striking Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex with missiles, damaging Trains 4 and 6 (12.8 million tonnes/annum, 17% of Qatar's LNG exports). Pearl GTL facility also damaged. Estimated $20 billion in annual revenue loss; repair timeline 3-5 years.
- **March 20, 2026**: Iranian drone strikes hit Kuwait's Mina al-Ahmadi refinery (730,000 bpd capacity) for the second consecutive day, and Mina Abdullah refinery. Iraq declared force majeure on all foreign-operated oilfields. Basra Oil Company production cut from 3.3 mb/d to 900,000 bpd. Brent crude hit $112/bbl. Trump administration lifted sanctions on 140 million barrels of Iranian crude on vessels to ease prices.
- **March 21, 2026**: Trump threatened to strike Iran's power plants if Hormuz does not reopen by Monday. Brent at $107.40/bbl (down from $113.71 previous day).

#### Scale of Disruption

- **Oil flow through Hormuz before conflict**: ~20 mb/d (25% of world's seaborne oil trade)

- **Gulf production cuts**: At least 10 mb/d shut in by Gulf countries
- **Global supply plunge**: Projected 8 mb/d drop in March 2026
- **IEA assessment**: "Largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market"
- **Historical comparison**: Exceeds 1973 embargo (4.3 mb/d), 1979 Iranian Revolution (5.6 mb/d), and 1990 Gulf War disruptions

### 1.2 Russia-Ukraine War (Year 5)

#### Energy Infrastructure Attacks on Ukraine

Russia has systematically destroyed Ukraine's energy generation capacity:
- **Pre-invasion capacity**: 33.7 GW
- **Current capacity (Jan 2026)**: ~14 GW — a loss of ~60% of total generation
- **October 2025 – January 2026**: 256 drone and missile strikes logged on energy facilities: 11 on hydroelectric plants, 94 on thermal plants, 151 on substations
- Ukraine's energy minister stated: "Not a single power plant left in Ukraine that the enemy has not attacked"
- **Kyiv**: Requires 1,700 MW but has only ~850 MW available; residents face up to 16 hours/day without power
- **Kharkiv**: A major energy facility destroyed in January 2026, leaving 400,000 without electricity
- **Overall electricity consumption**: Down ~30% from pre-invasion levels
- Ukraine needs to install 2.7 GW of new generation capacity to meet basic needs
- Emergency electricity imports ordered (targeting 50% of consumption)

#### Ukrainian Strikes on Russian Energy Infrastructure

- Ukraine's long-range drone campaign strikes Russian oil/gas infrastructure "almost daily"
- **Estimated 10% of Russian refining capacity knocked offline** (disputed; some estimates range up to 38%)
- **Volgograd refinery**: Suspended oil processing in February 2026 after Ukrainian drone strike triggered fire; crude distillation unit (~140,000 bpd, 40% of facility capacity) shut down — first major 2026 refinery shutdown
- **Saratov refinery (Rosneft)**: Processing unit and vertical storage tank damaged
- **Bashneft refineries**: Drone attacks repelled in March 2026
- Repeated strikes on Ryazan, Novokuibyshevsk, Volgograd, and Saratov refineries kept significant capacity offline
- **Total financial damage**: Over 1 trillion rubles ($12.3 billion) in combined losses from drone strikes on oil facilities
- Russia's oil processing has fallen ~3% year-over-year despite the campaign
- Russia's western border regions (Belgorod Oblast) experienced weeks-long power outages, heating at only 50% capacity

#### Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant (ZNPP)

- Europe's largest nuclear power plant remains under Russian occupation
- **January 2, 2026**: Last remaining backup 330 kV power line damaged by military activity
- **January 19, 2026**: Line reconnected after repairs
- **February 10, 2026**: Switchgear and Ferrosplavnaya power line damaged by Ukrainian Armed Forces fire
- **March 6, 2026**: Backup 330 kV Ferosplavna-1 line reconnected after IAEA-brokered local ceasefire (the fifth such ceasefire)
- Both sides continue to blame each other for attacks near the facility
- IAEA maintains continuous on-site presence; Director General warns of ongoing nuclear safety risks

### 1.3 Middle East — Israel-Gaza-Lebanon

- **Gaza**: Israel continues daily bombardment, has closed all crossings and stopped humanitarian aid flow, violating the October 2025 ceasefire agreement
- **Lebanon (March 2, 2026 – present)**: Ongoing war between Israel and Hezbollah; 1,000+ killed, ~2,584 wounded, nearly 1 million displaced (19% of population). IDF conducting ground operations in southern Lebanon
- **Iran war casualties (as of Day 21)**: Over 18,000 Iranian civilians injured; 1,400+ total deaths; 204 children killed
- **Israel**: At least 90 attempted Iranian strikes, ~20 hitting civilian areas, at least 10 killed. Israeli offshore gas fields (Leviathan, Tamar) production suspended as precaution

### 1.4 Africa — Sudan, Libya, Nigeria

#### Sudan

- Civil war since April 2023 has devastated the energy sector
- **Heglig oil field** (Sudan's largest): Completely shut down in December 2025; all staff evacuated
- **Khartoum refinery** (largest refinery, supplied most domestic diesel/gasoline/LPG): Completely non-operational
- Pipelines, refineries, and storage facilities damaged by attacks, including oil depots in Port Sudan
- **Oil revenues fallen >50%** compared to pre-conflict levels
- **South Sudan impact**: Pipeline responsible for ~two-thirds of South Sudan's oil exports broke down in February 2026; repairs require months amid active combat
- Heavy dependence on imported fuel; severe shortages affecting civilian population

#### Libya

- **Current production**: ~1.38 mb/d (up from 1.14 mb/d in 2024; highest annual average in a decade at 1.37 mb/d in 2025)
- **2026 target**: 1.6 mb/d by end of year
- Relative political stability has attracted Western oil majors: Shell, BP, ExxonMobil, Eni, Repsol, TotalEnergies signed exploration agreements
- **Risks**: No unified government; NOC received no approved operating budget throughout 2025; debts to service companies accumulating
- February 2026 licensing round disappointed: 22 blocks offered, 44 applicants, 37 pre-qualified, only 5 blocks awarded
- **Current assessment**: Production relatively stable but underlying political fragility persists

#### Nigeria

- Significant improvement in pipeline security compared to prior years
- **June 2025**: Achieved 100% crude oil pipeline availability for the first time
- **February 2026**: Armed Forces destroyed 32 illegal refining sites, recovered 175,000+ litres of stolen petroleum products under Operation DELTA SAFE
- **Historical context**: Just three years ago, only 30% of oil in some pipelines reached destination
- Oil infrastructure attacks from Niger Delta militants historically deterred Big Oil investment
- **Current assessment**: Most improved situation among conflict-affected producers, though vigilance required

## 3. Shipping Route Disruptions

### 3.1 Strait of Hormuz (CRITICAL — EFFECTIVELY CLOSED)

- **Normal traffic**: ~20 mb/d of crude oil and oil products (25% of world's seaborne oil trade); ~20% of global LNG supply
- **Current status (as of Mar 21, 2026)**: Effectively closed since Mar 2, 2026. Only 21 tankers transited since Feb 28 vs. 100+ daily pre-conflict
- **Iranian methods**: Drone strikes, explosive-laden unmanned surface vessels, fewer than 10 naval mines, IRGC fast-attack craft, GPS jamming
- **Bypass capacity**: Saudi East-West Pipeline (Petroline) ramped to ~4.2 mb/d by Mar 18, heading toward 7 mb/d emergency max; UAE ADCOP pipeline (Abu Dhabi-Fujairah) provides additional ~1.5 mb/d
- **Net shortfall**: Total bypass capacity of 3.5-5.5 mb/d vs. 20 mb/d pre-conflict flow = 14.5-16.5 mb/d net gap
- **Insurance**: War-risk insurance premiums at levels not seen since the 1980s Tanker War
- **Shipping rates**: Clean vessel charter rates climbed to $200,000/day; diesel premium expansion 2.3x vs. crude increases

### 3.2 Red Sea / Suez Canal

- **Houthi attacks (2023-2024)**: 178 vessels attacked during two-year blockade; 4 ships sunk, 9 sailors killed
- **2025 pause**: Houthis paused attacks in early 2025
- **Current status (Mar 2026)**: Houthis threatened to resume attacks on Feb 28 following Iran war escalation, but as of mid-March have shown unexpected restraint — no attacks launched
- **Risk assessment**: Strategic pause, not capacity degradation; Houthis waiting for Iranian coordination
- **Critical risk**: If Houthis resume attacks while Saudi Arabia reroutes 4.2+ mb/d through Yanbu (Red Sea port), both the Strait of Hormuz AND Red Sea routes would be compromised simultaneously — a catastrophic scenario
- **Saudi vulnerability**: Saudi Arabia's Red Sea oil reroute is now the lifeline for global oil supply; any Red Sea disruption would be devastating

### 3.3 Alternative Routing Costs

- Saudi Arabia rerouting through 1,200-km East-West Pipeline (Petroline) to Yanbu: capacity ramping from 2.2-2.5 mb/d to 4.2 mb/d (target: 7 mb/d)
- Iraq reviving Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline (Turkey) to bypass Hormuz: 200,000-250,000 bpd capacity. Pipeline restarted March 17, 2026
- Iraq's Basra production slashed from 3.3 mb/d to 900,000 bpd (for domestic refinery use only)
- Longer shipping routes around Africa add 10-15 days and significant fuel costs
- Jet fuel surcharges reaching 150-200% above normal levels

## 4. Sanctions & Trade Restrictions

### 4.1 Russia Oil/Gas Sanctions

#### Current Sanctions Architecture
- **EU oil product ban**: Ban on imports of oil products made from Russian crude effective January 21, 2026
- **EU LNG ban**: Prohibition on Russian LNG imports effective April 25, 2026 (except pre-June 2025 contracts >1 year)
- **UK**: Import ban on oil products refined from Russian-origin crude in third countries (effective Jan 21, 2026)
- **US sanctions on Rosneft and Lukoil**: Caused 83% year-on-year drop in seaborne crude exports from Dec 2025 to Feb 2026 (totaling 3.5 million tonnes)
- **Shadow fleet**: In Feb 2026, exactly half of Russian sea-borne oil transported by sanctioned "shadow" tankers

#### Sanctions Rollback Due to Iran War

- **March 13-14, 2026**: Trump administration issued 30-day waiver allowing purchase of sanctioned Russian oil stranded at sea
- **Volume affected**: ~7.3 million barrels in floating storage + 148.6 million barrels in transit (Russia claims ~100 million barrels, nearly a day's global output)
- **EU reaction**: European Council president called it "very concerning"; German Chancellor Merz said "easing sanctions now is wrong"
- **Ukraine's President Zelenskyy**: Warned move "could provide Russia with around $10 billion for the war"
- **March 20, 2026**: Similar waiver issued for 140 million barrels of Iranian crude on vessels

#### Sanctions Evasion

- Refineries in India, Turkey, Brunei, and Georgia processing Russian crude exported €567 million of oil products to sanctioning countries in Feb 2026
- EU imported €221 million, Australia €193 million, US €153 million of these products
- Shadow fleet remains active despite sanctions

### 4.2 Iran Oil Sanctions

- **Pre-war**: US maintained comprehensive sanctions on Iranian oil exports; China was primary buyer via shadow banking network
- **February 2026**: US imposed new sanctions on illicit traders of Iranian oil and the shadow fleet
- **March 20, 2026**: Trump administration lifted sanctions on 140 million barrels of Iranian crude already loaded on vessels — described as "narrowly tailored" and "temporary" by Treasury Secretary Bessent
- **Paradox**: US is effectively "funding a war against itself" — easing sanctions on Iranian oil while conducting military operations against Iran
- **Rationale**: Desperate attempt to contain energy prices as Brent approached $120/bbl

### 4.3 Venezuela — Sanctions Relief for Production

- **Current production**: ~800,000 bpd (vs. peak of 3.5 mb/d in 1990s)
- **2026 projection**: 30-40% increase possible (~300,000-400,000 bpd additional)
- Trump administration issued licenses allowing Western oil firms to operate in Venezuela following capture of Maduro
- US-Venezuela oil deal on track to hit $2 billion
- Full recovery to 3 mb/d would take at least a decade and hundreds of billions in investment

### 4.4 Impact on Global Supply

- **Total estimated supply disruption**: ~10 mb/d from Gulf production cuts + additional disruptions
- **IEA emergency release**: 400 million barrels from strategic reserves (US contributing 172 million barrels from SPR; delivery over ~120 days)
- **Buffer duration**: Emergency supplies provide 73-83 days of buffer at current disruption levels
- Previous largest release: 183 million barrels after Russia's 2022 Ukraine invasion
- **OPEC+ spare capacity**: Limited additional volumes available outside of Gulf states already affected
- Non-OPEC+ producers (US, Brazil, Guyana, Canada) expected to provide 1.1 mb/d additional supply in 2026

## 6. Major Power Energy Strategies

### 6.1 China's Energy Position

- **Oil imports**: 11.6 mb/d in 2025 (up from 11.1 mb/d in 2024); boosted imports ~16% in early 2026
- **Strategic stockpiling**: Planning 169 million barrels of new storage capacity in 2025-2026; EIA expects 1 mb/d stockpile expansion in 2026
- **Energy self-sufficiency**: 84.4% — highest among major importers
- **Land-based supply**: China-Russia and China-Kazakhstan crude oil pipelines immune to maritime blockades
- **Critical vulnerability**: Only 35-day gas reserve capacity (vs. Japan's 254 days)
- **Iran war impact**: War squeezing China's oil lifeline, but overland routes provide resilience. Iran still shipping oil to China through Hormuz under the de facto blockade
- **Clean energy hedge**: Target 25% non-fossil fuel share by 2030 (21.7% in 2025); global solar/wind capacity to reach 4,000 GW in 2026

### 6.2 India's Role

- **Russian oil share**: ~35% of total oil imports (top supplier); one-third of imports from 2024-2026
- **Volume**: March 2026 seaborne Russian crude imports on track for ~1.61 mb/d; previous month hit record 2.07 mb/d
- **US waiver**: Indian companies bought all available Russian crude on spot market within one week of US granting temporary purchasing waiver
- **Policy stance**: "Energy security of 1.4 billion Indians is the supreme priority" — rejects need for US permission
- **Vulnerability**: Only 20-25 day refinery inventory (operational capacity)
- **Impact**: India is a critical swing buyer, redirecting global flows; post-waiver imports could touch 2 mb/d+

### 6.3 NATO Energy Security

- **2025 Hague Summit**: Allies agreed to raise defense spending to 5% of GDP by 2035, with up to 1.5% annually for critical infrastructure protection
- NATO Energy Security Centre of Excellence (ENSEC COE) conducting Operational Level Energy Security Course (March 9-13, 2026, Tartu, Estonia)
- Focus on fuel interoperability: shared standards so allied forces can "plug and play" using any ally's energy systems
- Baltic Sentry operation protecting undersea infrastructure in Baltic Sea

## 7. Escalation Scenarios

### 7.1 Scenario: Prolonged Hormuz Closure (1-3 months)

- **IEA reserves**: 400 million barrels provide 73-83 days of buffer at current disruption levels
- **Economic impact**: 2.5-3 percentage points added to global CPI; stagflation dilemma for central banks
- **Regional vulnerability hierarchy**:
  - **Tier 1 (Japan/South Korea)**: Japan holds 254 days reserves; South Korea 208 days. Stock markets already down 8.6% (Nikkei) and 12% (Kospi)
  - **Tier 2 (China)**: 84.4% self-sufficient with overland alternatives; 35-day gas reserve is acute weakness
  - **Tier 3 (Taiwan/India)**: Taiwan has only 11 days of LNG supply (cliff risk; TSMC consumes 9-10% of electricity); India has 20-25 day refinery inventory
- **Semiconductor risk**: 90%+ of global sulfur is oil refining byproduct; shortage threatens semiconductor etching capability
- **Mining cascade**: Australia's 40% diesel consumption in mining threatens iron ore production (75% of China's seaborne imports)

### 7.2 Scenario: Houthi Resumption + Red Sea Closure

- Would block both Hormuz AND the Saudi Petroline bypass route through Yanbu/Red Sea
- Eliminates remaining 3.5-5.5 mb/d bypass capacity
- Creates total energy isolation of Gulf producers
- Only outlet: Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline (Iraq-Turkey) at 200,000-250,000 bpd — woefully insufficient

### 7.3 Scenario: Escalation to Iranian Power Plants / Nuclear Facilities

- Trump threatened to strike Iran's power plants if Hormuz not reopened by Monday (March 24)
- Bushehr nuclear plant already hit within 350 meters of reactor (Mar 18)
- IAEA Director General: attacks near nuclear plants violate all seven safety/security pillars
- Rosatom evacuated ~250 Russian employees/families from Bushehr; ~480 remain
- Iran's Natanz nuclear facility confirmed damaged by IAEA (Mar 3)
- Ground war in Iran being actively discussed

### 7.4 Scenario: Systemic Energy Market Breakdown

### Structural Shifts Underway

1. **Energy trade rerouting**: Most dramatic restructuring since 1973 embargo. Saudi Arabia pioneering Hormuz bypass via Petroline
2. **Sanctions architecture crumbling**: US forced to ease both Russian and Iranian sanctions simultaneously to manage prices
3. **Strategic reserves depletion**: IEA members drawing down at unprecedented rate; US SPR faces 120-day delivery timeline
4. **Accelerated energy transition**: War expected to speed adoption of nuclear power, renewables, and diversification away from fossil fuels
5. **New alliances**: India-Russia energy axis deepening; China securing overland routes; Gulf states reassessing security arrangements
6. **Global investment**: $3.3 trillion in energy investment (2025); two-thirds flowing to clean energy — trend accelerating due to security concerns

### Outlook

The 2026 energy crisis is fundamentally different from previous disruptions in its scale (10+ mb/d vs. 4-6 mb/d historically), geographic scope (simultaneous disruptions across Middle East, Eastern Europe, and Africa), and cascading nature (oil, gas, LNG, petrochemicals, and shipping all affected simultaneously). The duration of the Hormuz closure is the single most important variable determining whether the crisis remains manageable or becomes systemically destructive. If resolved within 1-3 months, strategic reserves and bypass routes can absorb the shock. Beyond 3 months, non-linear economic cascades — particularly in LNG-dependent Asia and petrochemical-dependent semiconductor supply chains — threaten global recession.
