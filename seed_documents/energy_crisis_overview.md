## 3. War Impacts on Energy

### Iran-Israel/US War (Operation Epic Fury)

**Scale of Disruption:**
- IEA declared this "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market"
- 20 million barrels per day previously transited the Strait of Hormuz; reduced to "a trickle"
- 8 million barrels per day predicted global supply loss in March 2026
- 10 million barrels daily subtracted from production due to Middle Eastern country shut-ins (Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Iraq, UAE)
- Approximately 20% of global daily oil supply affected
- One-fifth of global LNG trade disrupted

**Strait of Hormuz Blockade:**
- March 2, 2026: IRGC declares strait closed to "unfriendly nations"
- March 4, 2026: IRGC claims "complete control" of the strait
- Only 21 tankers transited since February 28 vs. 100+ ships daily before the conflict
- Tanker traffic initially dropped ~70%, with 150+ ships anchoring outside the strait
- Traffic subsequently dropped to approximately zero for non-Iranian vessels
- Iran continues shipping its own oil to China through the strait
- One-fifth of globally traded crude oil normally passes through the strait daily

**Bypass Capacity (Severely Limited):**
- Saudi Arabia East-West pipeline to Yanbu: 2.5 million b/d available (5 mb/d total capacity, half already in use)
- UAE bypass capacity: 500,000 b/d
- Combined bypass: ~3 million b/d against 20 million b/d halted exports
- Result: 85% of volumes remain stranded
- Bahrain, Iraq, Kuwait, and Qatar have zero bypass capability
- Saudi Aramco rerouting via Yanbu on Red Sea; daily loadings at port more than doubled

**Global Economic Impact:**
- Oil prices rose ~45% since the war began (late February to mid-March)
- Crude oil topped $110/barrel, peaked at $126 (Brent), $166 (Dubai crude, record)
- Global equities lost over 5% since late February
- Analysts estimate $30/barrel price increase could slow US growth by 0.3-0.5 percentage points of GDP
- Prolonged closure could push oil toward $170/barrel (10% reduction in global consumption)
- Central banks face stagflation risks; inflation pressures limit rate-cutting options
- Food security crisis: Gulf imports 77% of rice, 89% of corn, 95% of soybeans; one-quarter of global fertilizer production transits the strait

### Russia-Ukraine War Energy Impacts

**Ukraine's Energy Sector (Devastated):**
- Ukraine has lost approximately 70% of its power generation capacity
- Available generating capacity: fallen from 33.7 GW (start of invasion) to ~14 GW (January 2026)
- Many civilians receive only 3-4 hours of electricity daily
- Kyiv has about half the electricity it needs
- Ukraine's energy minister: "not a single power plant left in Ukraine that the enemy has not attacked"
- Ukraine needs to install up to 2.7 GW of new capacity by end of 2026

**European Energy Decoupling from Russia:**
- EU imports of Russian gas (pipeline + LNG): dropped to ~13% (from 45% in 2021)
- EU imports of Russian oil: dropped below 3% (from 27% in 2021)
- January 2026: EU Council adopted regulation prohibiting Russian LNG and pipeline gas imports from March 18, 2026
- All Russian gas imports to EU to be prohibited by end of 2027
- EU energy commissioner warns returning to Russian energy would be a "strategic blunder"

**Russia's Economic Position:**
- Hydrocarbon revenues: roughly one-third of federal budget income (down from 50% in 2010s)
- Military/security expenditures: over one-third of state budget (up to 40% including all security costs)
- Trump administration temporarily eased Russian sanctions during Iran war, generating ~$150 million/day in additional revenue for Russia "with little visible effect on global oil prices"

### Red Sea / Houthi Disruptions

**Current Status (March 2026):**
- Despite the Iran war, Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping have paradoxically paused
- No Houthi attacks on vessels for three weeks since the Iran war began (baffling experts)
- In mid-February 2026, container shipping had begun returning to the Red Sea (ME11 service resumed via Suez Canal)

**Residual Risks:**
- 30 tankers near Saudi port city of Yanbu currently within Houthi strike range
- The Bab el-Mandeb strait remains the only viable oil route from Saudi Red Sea ports
- If Houthis resume attacks and "completely trap" crude supply, Brent could soar to $130-$150/barrel
- Aramco redirecting millions of barrels via Yanbu pipeline — this route is now critical but also vulnerable

**Previous Red Sea Crisis (2024-early 2026):**
- Houthi attacks disrupted global shipping from late 2023 through early 2026
- Container lines rerouted around the Cape of Good Hope, adding weeks to transit times
- Some normalization occurred in early 2026 before the Iran war complicated the picture

## 4. Supply and Demand Analysis

### OPEC+ Production Decisions

**Current Production Cuts:**
- 3.24 million barrels per day of cuts still in place (approximately 3% of global demand)
- Eight key participants: Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, Oman
- November 2, 2025: Reaffirmed pause on production increments for Q1 2026 (February-March)
- December 2025: Announced plan to return 2.2 million b/d gradually over 18 months starting April 2026

**March 2026 Decision:**
- OPEC+ maintained vast majority of production cuts with minor adjustments
- Paved way for "gradual, measured return of output starting Q2 2026"
- Unwinding described as "flexible and conditional on market conditions"
- Full conformity and compensation for overproduction since January 2024 reaffirmed
- However, the Iran war has fundamentally altered the supply landscape — actual Gulf output has been forcibly reduced by conflict far beyond voluntary OPEC+ cuts

### US Oil Production

**2026 Forecast:**
- Average production: 13.6 million barrels per day (EIA March 2026 STEO)
- Slight decrease expected compared to 2025 record levels
- Higher oil prices from Iran crisis leading to upward revisions in production expectations

**Shale Output:**
- October 2025: 9.04 million b/d (65% of total US production)
- Permian Basin: primary growth driver; Jefferies forecasts 66,000 b/d annual growth in 2026
- Bakken and Eagle Ford: showing signs of maturation with declining well productivity
- Modest gains expected in Alaska, Federal Gulf of Mexico, and parts of Permian
- Gains not sufficient to offset declines in mature basins

**Why Higher Prices Won't Spur Rapid US Production Growth:**
- Shale companies focused on capital discipline and shareholder returns
- Mature basins experiencing declining productivity
- Labor and equipment constraints
- Long lead times for new drilling programs

### Global Demand Trends

**Demand Growth Slowing:**
- IEA cut 2026 demand growth forecast to 0.6 million b/d
- World oil demand projected to expand by 0.9 million b/d (J.P. Morgan estimate)
- IEA previously forecast peak oil demand at 2029 (~106 million b/d); may already have occurred
- Iran war is both curbing oil demand directly (economic slowdown) and accelerating the shift away from oil

**Key Demand Centers:**
- China: nearly 6 million b/d of imports; heavily exposed to Gulf disruptions
- India, South Korea, Japan: major importers through the Strait of Hormuz
- 75% of the world's population lives in net fossil fuel-importing countries
- Net importers spent $1.7 trillion on fossil fuels in 2024
- For every $10/barrel oil price increase, global import costs rise ~$160 billion annually

### Strategic Petroleum Reserve Response

**US SPR Release:**
- Current SPR inventory: approximately 415 million barrels (up from 395 million one year ago)
- SPR capacity: 714 million barrels (current fill: ~58%)
- President Trump authorized release of 172 million barrels
- First tranche: 86 million barrels (DOE issued Request for Proposal)
- Deliveries from Bryan Mound, West Hackberry, and Bayou Choctaw facilities
- Expected timeline: ~120 days for full delivery
- Companies must return borrowed oil plus a premium

**Coordinated International Response:**
- IEA coordinated largest reserve release in history: 400 million barrels across member nations
- Represents one-third of readily available strategic reserves globally
- Yields ~3+ million barrels daily — far below the Hormuz disruption of 20 million b/d
- Described as "temporary relief lasting only weeks"

### Why US LNG Cannot Fill the Gulf Gap

- Terminals already at maximum capacity; no room for immediate expansion
- Building new liquefaction facilities takes years
- Most US LNG sold on 20-year contracts under "free on board" pricing (buyers control destination)
- Some cargo diversion to Asia represents reallocation, not increased supply
- US gas prices remain insulated (~$3/MMBtu vs $19/MMBtu global), contradicting ability to increase exports
- Gulf LNG disruption (10.4-11.5 Bcf/d normally transiting Hormuz) dwarfs US expansion capacity

- Deferred maintenance increases vulnerability
- Extreme weather: most common cause of energy disruptions; climate change exacerbating risk

## 10. Renewable Energy and the Oil Crisis

### Acceleration of Transition

- UN climate chief: war-driven price spikes highlight value of renewables
- "Sunlight doesn't depend on narrow and vulnerable shipping straits"
- IEA peak oil demand forecast (previously 2029) may already be occurring in 2026
- Crisis both curbing oil demand (economic slowdown) and sharpening incentive to leave oil

### Electric Vehicle Impact

- Global EV fleet avoided 1.7 million barrels/day of oil in 2025 (up from 1.3M in 2024)
- EV oil displacement = 70% of Iran's annual oil exports
- 39 countries now have EV sales exceeding 10% (up from 4 in 2019)
- China: 50%+ EV sales share in 2025; saving $28+ billion annually
- Vietnam (38%) and Uruguay (27%) exceed EU average (26%)

### Solar and Renewables Economics

- Solar panel prices halved since 2022
- Dispatchable solar (panel + battery): $76/MWh for tariff-free imports
- Battery prices fell 36% since 2022
- 2025 solar growth alone could displace gas-fired electricity equivalent to all Hormuz LNG (~600 TWh)
- Competitive economics now favor renewables over LNG as Asia's transition fuel

### Long-Term Oil Demand Trajectory

- Oil prices likely to trend toward $40-60/barrel in 2030s (not from security but from declining relevance)
- Replacing imported oil for road transport with EVs: reduces importers' bills by one-third (~$600 billion/year)
- Scaling EVs, solar, heat pumps: could reduce fossil fuel import bills by ~70%
- LNG investment case weakening as renewables become cheaper
