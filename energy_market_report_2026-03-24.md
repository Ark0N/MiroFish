## Price Discovery and Market Cascade Mechanisms

**Immediate Price Discovery in Crude Futures Markets**

The simulated energy crisis reveals that crude oil price discovery occurs with remarkable speed through global futures exchanges. As the simulation demonstrates, **crude oil futures (Brent, WTI) are traded on ICE/NYMEX with immediate price discovery.** When supply shocks trigger market disruptions, these benchmarks respond instantaneously—Brent Crude trading on ICE Futures Europe in London and WTI on NYMEX at Cushing, Oklahoma become the first price signals transmitted globally. The physical price assessment for Brent, known as Dated Brent and published by Platts (S&P Global Commodity Insights), follows futures pricing within hours, establishing the baseline for all downstream market repricing.

Natural gas markets exhibit similarly rapid price discovery mechanisms. **Natural gas spot markets (TTF, Henry Hub, JKM) experience immediate price discovery.** The Title Transfer Facility (TTF) in the Netherlands, Henry Hub in Louisiana, and the Japan Korea Marker (JKM) all adjust within the same trading day as crude shocks materialize. This parallel discovery process in both oil and gas creates a synchronized first-order impact across energy markets within days 1-7 of supply disruptions.

**Shipping and Tanker Rate Cascades**

The cascade mechanism accelerates through shipping markets with striking immediacy. **Tanker rates (BDTI) experience immediate booking surge in response to oil/gas price shocks.** The Baltic Dirty Tanker Index, which tracks crude oil tanker rates, spikes as traders and logistics operators rush to secure vessel capacity in response to price volatility. This represents a critical first-order transmission mechanism—shipping costs amplify energy price shocks by creating additional supply-side constraints. When crude prices spike, the cost to transport that crude increases simultaneously, compounding the price impact for refineries and importers worldwide.

**Refining Margins and Downstream Product Pricing**

The simulation reveals that refining operations serve as the critical nexus where upstream price shocks transmit to consumer-facing markets. **Refining margins (crack spreads) transmit oil price changes to consumer markets.** The 3-2-1 crack spread—representing the margin from processing 3 barrels of crude oil into 2 barrels of gasoline and 1 barrel of distillate—becomes the mechanism through which oil price volatility reaches gasoline pumps, diesel markets, and jet fuel contracts. **Temporal Advantage Windows and Market Repricing Cascades**

The simulation reveals distinct temporal windows where early-warning detection translated into first-mover advantages. **Tanker rates (BDTI) experience immediate booking surge in response to oil/gas price shocks**, with shipping market participants detecting supply disruptions through vessel booking patterns and port congestion signals within hours of crude futures price discovery. However, agents with advance knowledge of supply disruptions could position in tanker rate derivatives and shipping stocks days before the BDTI index itself spiked, capturing outsized returns during the repricing cascade.

The critical insight from the simulation is that information asymmetries persisted across distinct agent categories, with supermajors and integrated trading houses possessing days-to-weeks of advance notice compared to external commodity traders and financial market participants. This created a hierarchical structure of first-mover advantages: production companies knew supply disruptions first, integrated trading houses detected signals through physical cargo networks second, investment bank research teams published forecasts third, and broader financial markets repriced last. Each temporal advantage window enabled agents with superior information to position ahead of public price discovery, capturing outsized returns during the cascade repricing process.
